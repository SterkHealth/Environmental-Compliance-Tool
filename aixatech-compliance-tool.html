01 · Context

Why this tool, why now

A wave of hard regulation — led by the EU Deforestation Regulation, the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive, and nature-related disclosure frameworks — is forcing companies to prove the environmental footprint of their supply chains. For the first time, ignorance is not a legal defence.

African commodity exporters and their buyers are most exposed. Cocoa, coffee, timber, rubber, soy, palm — the products that power EU import markets are precisely the ones being scrutinised. Buyers are scrambling for a credible way to demonstrate compliance. Suppliers have no easy path to provide it.

The insight from Agvesto's founder
"Fear sells." The commercial urgency is regulatory-driven. Companies are not buying compliance tools because they love the planet — they're buying them because they are terrified of fines, supply chain bans, and being locked out of EU export markets. The demand is there. It does not need to be manufactured.

Aixatech holds the hardest piece: high-resolution, ground-truthed African environmental and agri-data that no incumbent has built. This is the data infrastructure on which a compliance product can be built. Agvesto holds the other piece: the relationships across the agri-value chain and a farming customer base that needs exactly this tool to sell into European markets.

02 · Product Vision

What the tool does

At its core: a company uploads or connects its supplier and farm list. The tool maps each entity geospatially, runs it against satellite land-use data, deforestation alerts, and protected area registries, then scores compliance against the applicable regulations — and generates audit-ready documentation.

04 · Regulatory Tailwinds

The laws driving demand

These are the primary compliance obligations creating urgent demand for this tool. The EUDR is the sharpest near-term forcing function — non-compliant African suppliers risk losing access to the EU market entirely.

EU Deforestation
Regulation (EUDR)
Requires companies to prove commodities (cocoa, coffee, timber, rubber, soy, palm, cattle) were not produced on land deforested after December 31, 2020. Traceability to GPS plot level is mandatory. Applies to EU importers and exporters supplying into the EU.
High urgency
EU CSRD
Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive requires large companies to disclose material nature and biodiversity risks in their value chains. Supply chain environmental data goes from voluntary to mandatory disclosure.
High urgency
TNFD Framework
Taskforce on Nature-related Financial Disclosures — a voluntary but rapidly mainstreaming framework requiring companies and financial institutions to assess and disclose nature-related risks. Increasingly expected by investors and lenders.
Medium urgency
UK Environment Act
Bans UK businesses from using key commodities produced on illegally deforested land. Similar in intent to EUDR — creates parallel compliance burden for UK-market suppliers.
Medium urgency
African National
Environmental Laws
Kenya, Ghana, Côte d'Ivoire, Nigeria and others have existing environmental protection statutes that suppliers must comply with domestically. Often unenforced but increasingly relevant as global ESG due diligence filters back to source countries.
Emerging
05 · Target Customers

Who buys this

The tool has three distinct customer segments. Each has a different entry point, different willingness to pay, and a different fear driving the purchase.

Primary
Commodity Traders & Food Companies
Sourcing African commodities (cocoa, coffee, soy, timber) for EU or UK markets. EUDR creates an existential compliance problem — they need traceability infrastructure fast or they lose market access.

Primary
Agribusiness Lenders & Investors
Banks and DFIs with agri-lending portfolios face TNFD and CSRD disclosure obligations. They need environmental risk scores on their loan books — and they have the budget to pay for data infrastructure.
IFC Rabobank AfDB Private agri-funds
Secondary
African Exporters & Cooperatives
The farmers and cooperatives themselves need to generate EUDR-compliant certificates to sell into the EU. Agvesto's existing customer base. Willingness to pay is lower — model may be B2B2C via the buyer paying.
Coffee cooperatives Cocoa boards Outgrower schemes
The smartest go-to-market
Sell to the EU importer first. They have the compliance obligation, the budget, and the leverage over the supply chain. They will mandate that their African suppliers use the tool — creating a pull-through effect that onboards the farming tier without a separate sales motion.
06 · Business Model

How it makes money

Revenue Stream 1
Data API
Aixatech licenses its African environmental data layer to the product as a recurring API fee. Priced per API call or per geography covered. This is Aixatech's cleanest revenue model — infrastructure-as-a-service.
Revenue Stream 2
SaaS Platform
Agvesto sells the compliance dashboard as a subscription to commodity traders and lenders. Tiered by number of suppliers monitored, geographies covered, and reporting outputs. Annual contract, enterprise pricing.
Revenue Stream 3
Compliance Certificates
Transaction-based revenue for generating individual EUDR due diligence certificates per lot or shipment. Particularly relevant for cooperatives and exporters — a per-certificate fee that scales with trade volume.
Revenue Stream 4
Insurance Bundle
The natural Agvesto overlay: a farmer who passes the compliance screen is also a better-understood insurance risk. Compliance tool + parametric insurance sold as a bundle is a differentiating offer no incumbent has.
Revenue Stream 5
Government / DFI
National governments and development finance institutions pay for country-level compliance infrastructure — particularly where a government wants to certify its entire cocoa or coffee sector as EUDR-compliant at the national level.
Defensibility
The moat
The data layer is the moat. An EU compliance tool built on European satellite data has no edge in African farmscapes. Aixatech's ground-truth African data is what makes this product accurate — and impossible to replicate quickly.

08 · Open Questions

What we need to resolve

Before this moves from concept to term sheet, these are the questions worth working through together.

Q1
Which geography first? EUDR urgency is sharpest for West African cocoa/coffee (Ghana, Côte d'Ivoire). Does Agvesto have relationships there? Does Aixatech have coverage depth there?
Q2
Is there a named pilot customer? The conversation becomes immediately more serious if Agvesto can bring a committed EU importer or lender to the table. Who are they talking to?
Q3
Revenue split and data licensing structure? How does Aixatech get paid — flat API fee, rev share, equity stake? This needs to be structured before any build work starts.
Q4
Who owns the legal rule engine? Maintaining an up-to-date database of environmental law across African and EU jurisdictions is a significant ongoing cost. Who funds and owns that layer?
Q5
Build vs partner on the compliance layer? Companies like Satelligence, Satellogic, and South Pole already have EUDR compliance tools. Is this a build, a white-label play, or a differentiated wedge into a crowded space?
Q6
What's Agvesto's current build capacity? Do they have engineering resource to build the product layer, or are they expecting Aixatech to contribute engineering too? Needs clarifying before scope is agreed.
